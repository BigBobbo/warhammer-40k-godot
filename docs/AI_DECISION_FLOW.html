Warhammer 40,000 · Godot 4 AI opponent architecture Glass-box heuristic engine

How the AI thinks— every decision, in order, and why.

The computer opponent is not a neural network and not a language model. It is a hand-built utility-scoring engine: on its turn it photographs the board, gives every legal action a numeric score, and plays the highest one — narrating the options it rejected as it goes. A second, signal-driven layer watches your turn for stratagem opportunities. This page walks the whole flow, from the turn structure down to the exact weights, with the reasoning surfaced on screen.

The answer in brief

Your questions, answered directly

Four things you asked — is there a decision tree, is there an order, what is decided where, and why — answered up front. The rest of the page is the evidence.

// Is there a decision tree?
Yes — one per phase. Each phase runs an ordered priority ladder (handle mandatory sub-actions first), then falls through to a utility-scoring step that picks the best tactical action. So: a short tree whose leaves are a scoring function.
// Is there an order?
Three nested orders. The phase sequence (Command → Movement → Shooting → Charge → Fight → Scoring), the evaluate → decide → route → apply loop that repeats inside each phase, and the step order within a single phase's ladder.
// What is decided, and where?
One action at a time. Where to move, who to shoot, whether to charge, who fights first, which objective to grab — all in AIDecisionMaker.gd. Reactive stratagems (overwatch, re-rolls…) are decided in AIPlayer.gd during your turn.
// Why each choice?
Highest score wins. Every candidate gets score = Σ(weighted factors); the max is chosen (plus a little difficulty "noise"). The rejected options and their scores are logged — and drawn on the board — so you can see the reasoning.
One sentence to remember

The engine's core rule is "one action per call, recomputed from a fresh snapshot of the board." It never trusts a stale plan — every decision starts by re-reading the current state, which is what makes it robust to the thousands of rule interactions in 40k.

The big picture

Two loops: one for its turn, one for yours

Everything the AI does comes from one of two entry points. They are deliberately separate pieces of machinery.

Active loop · its turn

Pull model, frame-paced. A per-frame timer in AIPlayer._process() fires an evaluation, which asks the phase for the legal actions, hands them to the decision engine, and routes the single best action back into the game — then re-arms itself. This drives deploy, move, shoot, charge, fight, and score.

_evaluate_and_act → _execute_next_action → decide() → route_action → (repeat)

Reactive loop · your turn

Push model, signal-driven. When a phase begins, _connect_phase_stratagem_signals() wires the AI to that phase's reactive events. When you move, charge, or roll dice, a signal wakes a handler that decides whether to spend CP on a stratagem — Fire Overwatch, Command Re-roll, Heroic Intervention, and more.

phase signal → _on_*_opportunity → evaluate → maybe route_action

Why it matters

Because the AI's action re-enters the same validate → process → apply pipeline as a human's (execute_action() in BasePhase.gd), the AI is "just another action source." It can never do something the rules engine wouldn't let you do.

The order of things · part 3

The decision tree, made concrete

"Is there a decision tree?" — here it is. Every _decide_<phase> function is an ordered priority ladder: it resolves forced or special sub-actions first, in a fixed order, and only reaches the scoring step for the real tactical choice. Below is the actual ladder for the Movement phase (_decide_movement), the richest one.

The pattern generalises

Every phase has this shape — a few guard rungs, then a scoring leaf. Shooting resolves grenades and builds its focus-fire plan; Charge resolves overwatch/heroic-intervention windows then scores charge targets; Fight resolves stances and picks the activation order then scores fight targets. The tree is shallow; the intelligence is in the leaf. That leaf is next.

Target priority = expected damage × strategic value

When choosing what to shoot, charge, or fight, the engine multiplies two independent quantities. Expected damage is pure probability — attacks × P(hit) × P(wound) × P(unsaved) × damage, respecting cover and keywords. Strategic value (above) is how badly it wants that unit gone, regardless of any one weapon. A cheap weapon that reliably finishes a wounded, objective-holding character can out-score a big gun aimed at a fresh tank.

The one true optimiser — focus fire

Shooting is the only phase that plans the whole army at once. _build_focus_fire_plan pools every ranged weapon, builds a weapon × target expected-damage matrix, computes each enemy's "kill threshold" (its remaining wounds), then allocates fire by iterative marginal value: repeatedly commit the single weapon-to-target pairing that buys the most useful damage right now — heavily rewarding the shot that crosses a kill threshold (×2.5) and discounting overkill (×0.35). That is how it concentrates fire instead of spreading it thin. Movement, charge and fight are per-unit greedy picks with coordination bonuses layered on, rather than a single joint plan.

Right tool for the job — weapon/target matching

A multiplier nudges each weapon toward suitable prey. Weapons are classed anti-tank / anti-infantry / general; targets are classed vehicle-monster / elite / horde.

The modifiers that give it a personality

Every raw score is finally scaled by a small stack of multipliers, so the same board can be read aggressively or cagily depending on the situation:

TEMPO

Behind on VP → push harder (up to ×1.5, ×1.8 desperation from round 4). Ahead → conserve toward ×0.8. Reads player VP − enemy VP.

ARMY ARCHETYPE

Auto-detected once per game: Melee / Shooting / Elite / Balanced. A melee army gets ×1.25 aggression and a lower charge bar; a gunline gets ×1.3 survival and holds back.

ROUND & FACTION

Rounds 1–2 aggressive, 3 neutral, 4–5 objective-hoarding. Faction flavour: Orks, Custodes, Khorne and World Eaters charge more readily.

The reactive layer

Stratagems: deciding on your turn

The reactive loop is the AI spending Command Points in response to what you do. Each is a small expected-value gate: estimate the payoff (expected mortal wounds, expected hits, damage ÷ target health, probability of failure…), compare it to a threshold, and — crucially — refuse to spend the last CP unless the payoff is high, keeping a point in reserve for emergencies. There is no deep look-ahead here; it is disciplined heuristics.

Turning the dial

Four difficulty tiers

Difficulty is not a stat bonus — it gates which behaviours run and how much random "noise" is stirred into the scores. Easy short-circuits the whole engine to random legal actions; each tier up unlocks more of the machinery and tightens the optimisation.

Reshaping it without code

Weights, profiles & conditional rules

Every number on this page is a default. The engine reads each weight through get_param(name, default), which resolves an override chain — so you can give Player 1 and Player 2 entirely different personalities, or make the AI change its mind mid-game, all from JSON.

A conditional rule

A rule fires when its conditions (ANDed) match the live context — phase, round, VP gap, health, distance to enemy, on-objective, unit type… — and then override, multiply or add to any weight.

// "When behind on VP in round 4+,
//  push objectives 50% harder."
{
  "conditions": [
    { "type": "round_gte", "value": 4 },
    { "type": "vp_behind" }
  ],
  "actions": [
    { "type": "multiply",
      "param": "URGENCY_LATE_GAME_PUSH",
      "value": 1.5 }
  ]
}

The supporting cast

  • ProfileManager.gd — loads / validates the JSON personality files (a visual editor ships as ai-creator/index.html).
  • AIAbilityAnalyzer.gd — reads each unit's special rules and turns them into offensive / defensive multipliers the scorer can use (e.g. an aura leader raises a target's value; a Feel-No-Pain unit is discounted as harder to kill).
  • AIDifficultyConfig.gd — the tier gates and noise levels from the previous section (code, not JSON).
  • The decision log — press F10 to export every scored decision with its full breakdown, for the external AI Gameplay Visualizer.

Straight talk

What it is, what it isn't

To set expectations honestly — and because you asked what's really going on:

It is

A layered, deterministic heuristic engine with genuine army-level coordination: focus-fire planning, a per-turn multi-phase plan, mission-card awareness, archetype detection and round/tempo pivots. Fully offline, instant, and always rule-legal because the game engine — not the AI — is the source of truth.

It isn't

A machine-learning model. There is no neural network, no training, no LLM. A per-action language model was explicitly rejected by the project: 50–200 engine actions per turn would mean minutes and real money per game, and it would break determinism, offline play and rules-legality. A turn-level "strategist" LLM that merely nudges these existing parameters is noted as a possible future experiment behind a flag.

Known rough edges

From the project's own architecture review — worth knowing when the AI does something odd:

  • Save/load can shift its behaviour. The engine keeps eight planning caches (the multi-phase plan, focus-fire plan, archetype…) that aren't serialised, so a reloaded game re-derives them.
  • Its damage math is a private copy. The AI reimplements hit/wound/save probability rather than calling the shared rules engine, so its expectations can drift slightly from what the dice actually do.
  • Deployment is reactive, not a master plan — it places units one at a time without an army-wide deployment posture, and those weights aren't yet tunable.
  • It's a big monolith — ~19,700 lines of scoring in one file. The roadmap is to split it per phase, point its math at the shared engine, and add CP-budget planning and a deeper look-ahead at the top tier.
